\section{Results}

The B3/B4 closure used the consolidated B1+B2 ranking to build a final publication table (REP-01), plus significance tests (STAT-02) over the full-scale B2 raw data.

\subsection{Family-Level Summary}

Table~\ref{tab:b3_rep01_summary} summarizes average performance across all scenarios and missing-ratio levels. The best method within each family is reported in the rightmost column.

The family-level summary reveals a performance crossover: the Baseline family achieves the lowest average MAE (\ResBaselineMAE) and RMSE (\ResBaselineRMSE), while the TV/Time family achieves the lowest average DTW (\ResTVDTW). This crossover has a practical implication: the best family depends on how reconstruction quality is defined. If pointwise amplitude fidelity is the priority (e.g., BCI feature extraction), baseline and GSP methods are competitive. If temporal dynamics must be preserved (e.g., event-related analysis), TV/time methods offer a clear advantage.

\subsection{Statistical Significance (STAT-02)}

STAT-02 tests \emph{reject $H_0$} for the key family contrast TV/time vs.\ instant on both MAE ($p = $\StatFamilyMAEpval) and DTW ($p = $\StatFamilyDTWpval). This confirms that family-level differences are not explained by noise alone under the current protocol.

At the method level, the contrast between TRSS and BGSRP is significant in MAE ($p = $\StatTRSSBGSRPpval), while TRSS vs.\ \iterid{gsmooth} fails to reject $H_0$ ($p = $\StatTRSSGSPpval). This supports a scenario-aware interpretation: there is a real difference between families, but within the GSP and TV/time families the top methods are not easily separable by MAE alone.

\subsection{Scenario-Dependent Behavior}

Full per-scenario rankings are available in \iterid{results/b2_publication_topk_by_family_scenario.csv}. Across the \ResNScenarios~scenarios (\ResScenariosNames), the relative ranking of methods changes depending on the region and level of missing data (\ResMissingLevels). This sensitivity is expected: frontal electrodes share different functional correlations than occipital ones, so a graph constructed from spatial proximity may not capture the dominant structure in all regions.

\subsection{BGSRP Status}

The BGSRP method is currently treated as a \BGSRPStatus. The residual gap is quantified in \iterid{results/b2_publication_bgsrp_gap_residual.csv} and classified as \emph{accepted for this stage}. BGSRP results should be interpreted accordingly until full cross-stack equivalence is established.

\subsection{Engine v7 extension (\iterid{it71}--\iterid{it82}): few-electrode missing scenarios and full-signal analysis}
\label{sec:results_it71_it82_v7}

Engine v7 extends the validation protocol beyond ratio-only missingness by introducing fixed-count scenarios
(\iterid{1ch}, \iterid{2ch}, \iterid{3ch}) and a partial Phase 7 analysis contrasting instantaneous reconstruction
against full-signal reconstruction.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Phase 6 (\iterid{it71}--\iterid{it80}): mixed outcomes (2 GO, 8 NO-GO), with GO obtained in
  \iterid{it73} and \iterid{it74} on \iterid{mne_sample_proxy} for \iterid{1ch}/\iterid{2ch} missing cases.
  \item Phase 7 partial (\iterid{it81}--\iterid{it82}): both iterations are NO-GO under the statistical gate,
  indicating that robust superiority under the full-signal criterion is not yet established in the current setup.
  \item Interpretation: the TV/Time advantage remains strong in v6 proxy settings (\iterid{it61}--\iterid{it70}),
  but v7 reveals sensitivity to dataset configuration, graph choice, and low-count missing-channel regimes.
\end{itemize}

These v7 outcomes are retained as critical boundary evidence and should be used for limitations and robustness
discussion rather than as confirmatory evidence.
